01

What is NLP?

Definition

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a branch of AI that gives computers the ability to understand, interpret, and generate human language — like text or speech. Deep Learning uses neural networks to do this automatically without manual rules.

Think of it this way: humans learn language by hearing millions of sentences. Deep Learning models do the same — they read billions of sentences and learn patterns automatically.

Real World Example

When you type "Hey Siri, what's the weather?" — NLP is working. The phone converts your speech to text, understands that you're asking about weather (not food, not movies), finds your location, and replies in natural language. Every step involves deep learning.

Why is NLP hard?

🔀
Ambiguity
"I saw a man with a telescope" — who has the telescope? Language is often unclear.
🌍
Context matters
"Bank" means river bank or money bank depending on the sentence around it.
😊
Sarcasm & Tone
"Yeah, great job." can be sincere or sarcastic. Humans know; computers struggle.
📚
Infinite vocabulary
New words ("selfie", "meme") appear all the time. Models must handle unknowns.

Traditional NLP vs Deep Learning NLP

Traditional NLP (Rule-Based)

  • Humans write grammar rules manually
  • Dictionary-based lookups
  • Fails on new words or phrases
  • Doesn't understand context well

Deep Learning NLP

  • Learns patterns from massive data automatically
  • Understands context and meaning
  • Handles new words gracefully
  • State-of-the-art performance on all tasks
02

Text Preprocessing

Definition

Text Preprocessing is the process of cleaning and converting raw text into a format that a deep learning model can understand. Models can't read raw text — they need numbers. Preprocessing converts text to numbers step by step.

Example — The sentence "Running FAST is Fun!!!" gets preprocessed

Original: "Running FAST is Fun!!!"
After lowercasing: "running fast is fun!!!"
After removing punctuation: "running fast is fun"
After tokenization: ["running", "fast", "is", "fun"]
After removing stop words: ["running", "fast", "fun"]
After stemming: ["run", "fast", "fun"]

Key Preprocessing Steps

1. Tokenization Core Step

Splitting text into smaller units called tokens. A token can be a word, sub-word, or character. Most modern models use sub-word tokenization (like BPE — Byte Pair Encoding).

Example: "unhappiness" → ["un", "happiness"] so the model handles rare words by breaking them into known parts.

2. Stop Word Removal

Words like "is", "the", "a", "and" carry almost no meaning. Removing them reduces noise.

Example: "The cat is on the mat" → "cat mat" (after removing stop words)

3. Stemming vs Lemmatization Important

Stemming: Crudely chops word endings. "studies" → "studi" (not a real word, but fast)
Lemmatization: Converts to actual base word using grammar rules. "studies" → "study" (correct real word)

Use Lemmatization for better accuracy; Stemming for speed.

4. Vocabulary Building & Indexing

After tokenization, every unique token gets an integer ID. This is how text becomes numbers.

Example: {"cat": 1, "sat": 2, "mat": 3, "on": 4}
"cat sat on mat" → [1, 2, 4, 3]

💡 Note: Modern deep learning models (like BERT, GPT) often skip manual preprocessing and use learned tokenizers (like WordPiece, BPE) that handle all of this automatically during training.
03

Word Embeddings

Definition

Word Embeddings are dense numeric vectors that represent words. Instead of treating words as random integers, embeddings place words in a mathematical space where similar words are close together. This lets the model understand meaning, not just identity.

Example — Why embeddings are magical

Without embeddings: "king" = 5, "queen" = 8, "man" = 2. These numbers have no relationship.

With embeddings (Word2Vec): king - man + woman ≈ queen
The model learns that king and queen are similar, and it knows the "royalty" relationship. This is impossible with plain integer IDs.

Types of Word Embeddings

MethodHow it worksKey Advantage
One-Hot Encoding Each word = a vector of 0s with one 1. "cat" in vocab of 5 = [1,0,0,0,0] Simple, but no meaning — "cat" and "kitten" are equally far apart
Word2Vec Trained to predict context words around each word (CBOW or Skip-gram) Captures semantic relationships; king-queen analogy works
GloVe Uses word co-occurrence statistics across the whole corpus Better at capturing global meaning patterns
FastText Breaks words into character n-grams; learns sub-word info Handles misspellings and rare words well
Contextual Embeddings (BERT, GPT) Same word gets a different vector based on sentence context "bank" means different things in different sentences — handled correctly
💡 Key Insight: Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText give a FIXED vector per word regardless of context. But "bank" in "river bank" and "bank account" should have different meanings. This is why contextual embeddings (BERT, GPT) are so much more powerful.

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN)

Definition

A Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) is a type of neural network designed to handle sequential data — data where order matters. Unlike regular neural networks that process each input independently, RNNs have a "memory" — they pass information from one step to the next through a hidden state.

Example — Predicting the next word

Sentence: "The sky is ___"
An RNN reads: "The" → "sky" → "is" → now it uses memory of all previous words to predict the next one should probably be "blue" or "clear". A regular network without memory would see "is" and have no idea what came before.

How RNN Works — Step by Step

The Vanishing Gradient Problem

Big Problem

When training RNNs on long sequences, the gradients (signals for learning) become extremely tiny as they travel back in time. The model essentially "forgets" information from far back in the sentence. This is called the Vanishing Gradient Problem.

Example

Sentence: "I grew up in France, went to school there, learned the culture, studied the history... and now I speak fluent ___"

The answer is "French", but the clue "France" is 20 words ago. A basic RNN forgets it. This is why LSTMs were invented.

05

LSTM & GRU

Definition

Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) is an advanced RNN that solves the vanishing gradient problem by using special "gates" that control what information to remember, what to forget, and what to output. GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit) is a simpler, faster version of LSTM.

Analogy — LSTM is like a notebook with sticky notes

Imagine you're taking notes in class. With a plain RNN, you can only hold one note at a time — the new one replaces the old one. With LSTM, you have a notebook (cell state). You can decide what to erase (forget gate), add new notes (input gate), and show specific notes to others (output gate). Important info stays; irrelevant info is erased.

The Three LSTM Gates Explained

🚪 Forget Gate Erase

Decides what information to throw away from the cell state. Uses a sigmoid function that outputs 0 (forget everything) to 1 (keep everything).

Example: When a new subject starts in a conversation, the forget gate clears the previous topic from memory.

📝 Input Gate Add

Decides what new information to store in the cell state. Two parts: sigmoid layer decides which values to update, tanh layer creates new candidate values to add.

Example: When you read a new character's name in a story, the input gate stores it as important context.

📤 Output Gate Output

Decides what parts of the cell state to output as the hidden state (the "working memory" passed to the next step).

Example: When predicting the next word, the output gate selects only the relevant parts of memory to inform the prediction.

LSTM vs GRU

LSTM

  • Has 3 gates + separate cell state
  • More expressive for complex sequences
  • More parameters, slower training
  • Better for very long sequences

GRU

  • Only 2 gates (reset + update)
  • Simpler, fewer parameters
  • Faster to train
  • Comparable performance on most tasks

Seq2Seq & Encoder-Decoder Architecture

Definition

A Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq) model takes a sequence as input and produces a different sequence as output. It uses an Encoder (reads the input) and a Decoder (generates the output). This architecture powers machine translation, chatbots, and text summarization.

Example — English to French translation

Input: "How are you?"
The Encoder reads all 3 words and compresses the meaning into a single vector called the context vector (like a summary of the sentence).
The Decoder then reads this context vector and generates: "Comment vas-tu?" word by word.

Problem: One context vector has to carry ALL the information, which fails for long sentences.

The Architecture

Encoder
Reads input sequence word by word using LSTM/GRU. Produces context vector.
→
Context Vector
Fixed-size representation of the entire input. The "bottleneck" of information.
→
Decoder
Takes context vector and generates output sequence one word at a time.
⚠️ The Bottleneck Problem: Long sentences like a 50-word paragraph can't be compressed into one small vector without losing information. This is the key motivation for the Attention Mechanism (Section 7).
07

Attention Mechanism

Definition

The Attention Mechanism allows the decoder to "look back" at all encoder outputs (not just the final context vector) and decide which input words are most relevant for generating each output word. Instead of one bottleneck vector, the decoder can attend to the entire input sequence with varying weights.

Example — Translating "The black cat sat"

When the decoder is generating the French word for "cat" (= "chat"), it doesn't need to look at "The" or "sat" much. Instead, attention gives high weight to the word "cat" in the input, and low weight to the other words. Each output word gets its own personalized "focus" on the input. This is dramatically more accurate for long sentences.

How Attention Works

Step 1: Compute Attention Scores

For each decoder step, compute a score between the current decoder hidden state and every encoder hidden state. Higher score = more relevant. Formula: score(decoder_state, encoder_state) — usually a dot product or a small neural network.

Step 2: Softmax to Get Weights

Apply softmax to turn scores into probabilities (all weights sum to 1). These are called attention weights.
Example: [0.05, 0.8, 0.10, 0.05] — 80% focus on word 2 ("cat").

Step 3: Weighted Sum = Context

Multiply each encoder state by its attention weight and sum them up. This gives a context vector that's different for each output word — much richer than one fixed vector.

Self-Attention

Advanced Concept

Self-Attention (used in Transformers) applies attention within the same sequence — every word attends to every other word in the same sentence. This lets each word gather context from anywhere in the sentence, not just words before it.

Example — Self-attention on "The animal didn't cross the street because it was tired"

What does "it" refer to? "animal" or "street"? Self-attention helps the model figure out that "it" attends strongly to "animal" (because animals get tired, not streets). Without self-attention, this is very hard to get right.

Queries, Keys, and Values (Q, K, V)

Self-attention uses 3 learned vectors for each word:

Query (Q)
What this word is looking for. Like a search query. "What context do I need?"
Key (K)
What each word is offering. Like a database key. "What do I represent?"
Value (V)
The actual content. After attention weights are computed using Q and K, the values are summed up.
💡 Analogy: Think of a YouTube search. Your search term = Query. Video titles = Keys. Video content = Values. You match your query against keys to find relevant content, then you retrieve the actual values.
08

Transformers

Definition — The Most Important Architecture in Modern NLP

The Transformer (introduced in "Attention is All You Need", 2017) completely removed recurrence (RNNs) and relied entirely on self-attention. It processes all words in parallel (not one by one), making it drastically faster and better at capturing long-range dependencies.

Why Transformers changed everything

RNNs process words like reading a book one word at a time, left to right. Transformers process the entire sentence at once — every word simultaneously looks at every other word. This parallelism means you can train on much larger datasets much faster. GPT, BERT, ChatGPT, Gemini — they're all Transformers.

Transformer Architecture Components

1. Input Embedding + Positional Encoding Entry point

Tokens are converted to embeddings. But since attention has no built-in sense of order, Positional Encoding adds information about word position using sine/cosine waves. This tells the model: "This is word 1, this is word 2, etc."

Example: "Dog bites man" and "Man bites dog" have the same words. Positional encoding distinguishes them.

2. Multi-Head Attention Core Layer

Instead of one attention function, Transformers run attention multiple times in parallel (multiple heads). Each head can focus on a different type of relationship. One head might track subject-verb agreement, another might track pronoun references.

8 heads in the original Transformer, 12 in BERT-base.

3. Feed-Forward Neural Network

After attention, each token is passed through a small neural network independently. This adds non-linearity and increases the model's ability to transform representations. Same network applied to each token position.

4. Layer Normalization + Residual Connections Stability

Residual connections add the input back to the output (x + sublayer(x)). This prevents information from being lost as layers deepen. Layer Norm normalizes the values to stabilize training.

5. Encoder vs Decoder in Transformer Architecture choice

Encoder: Processes the full input. Used in BERT. Every word can attend to every other word (bidirectional).
Decoder: Generates output word by word. Used in GPT. Each word can only attend to previous words (causal/masked attention).
Both: Used in T5, original Transformer for translation tasks.

BERT, GPT & Pre-Trained Models

Definition

Pre-trained Language Models are Transformers trained on enormous text corpora (books, Wikipedia, web) first, and then fine-tuned for specific tasks. They've learned general language knowledge during pre-training, so fine-tuning on a small task-specific dataset is fast and effective.

Analogy — Pre-training is like going to school

BERT pre-trained on 3.3 billion words is like a student who spent years in school learning to read, write, reason, and understand language. Fine-tuning for sentiment analysis is like that student taking a short 1-week course on product reviews. They learn fast because they already know so much.

BERT — Bidirectional Encoder Representations

BERT (2018 by Google) Encoder-only

Uses only the Transformer Encoder. Reads text bidirectionally — every word sees all other words in both directions simultaneously.

Pre-training tasks:
1. Masked Language Modeling (MLM): Randomly mask 15% of words, predict them. Like a fill-in-the-blank game.
"The [MASK] sat on the mat" → "cat"

2. Next Sentence Prediction (NSP): Given two sentences, predict if the second follows the first naturally.

Best for: Classification, question answering, named entity recognition — any task where you understand a full input.

GPT — Generative Pre-trained Transformer

GPT (OpenAI) Decoder-only

Uses only the Transformer Decoder. Reads text left to right only (causal attention — each word only sees words before it).

Pre-training task: Predict the next word.
"The cat sat on the ___" → "mat"

GPT-2 → GPT-3 (175B parameters) → GPT-4. Each version is trained on more data with more parameters.

Best for: Text generation, chatbots, code generation, summarization — any task where you generate new text.

📌 Complete Summary — Everything in One Place

Text Preprocessing converts raw text to numbers (tokenize → index → embed).

Word Embeddings give words meaning as vectors. Similar words are close together. Contextual embeddings (BERT) adapt based on sentence context.

RNNs process sequences step by step with memory, but struggle on long sequences due to vanishing gradients.

LSTM & GRU solve this with gating mechanisms — selectively remember and forget information.

Seq2Seq uses encoder to compress input + decoder to generate output. Limited by the bottleneck of one context vector.

Attention lets the decoder focus on relevant parts of the input. Self-attention lets every word look at every other word in the same sequence.

Transformers use only attention (no RNNs), process all tokens in parallel, and are the foundation of all modern NLP.

BERT = bidirectional Transformer encoder, great for understanding. GPT = causal decoder, great for generating text.

Pre-trained models learn general language from massive data, then fine-tune cheaply for specific tasks.
